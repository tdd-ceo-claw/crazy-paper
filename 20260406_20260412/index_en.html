Monthly Weather Review (AMS)

Radar Data Assimilation with JEDI LETKF for Ensemble Forecasting of Hurricane Ida (2021) Using a HAFS-Like Configuration

Why it matters: A clean same-week example of radar observations feeding directly into hurricane ensemble prediction.

  • The abstract evaluates direct radar assimilation inside the JEDI Local Ensemble Transform Kalman Filter framework for Hurricane Ida.
  • Radial-velocity assimilation improves inner-core wind structure, reduces track spread during cycling, and improves landfall-location forecasts.
  • Reflectivity assimilation contributes more to rainfall skill, especially for extreme rainfall and outer-rainband precipitation before landfall.

Journal of Hydrometeorology (AMS)

Evaluation of Radar-Derived Polarimetric Precipitation Estimates for Extreme Rain Events Using a Dense Network of Rain Gauges

Why it matters: This is a practical radar-QPE paper tied to flood forecasting and gauge-based verification.

  • The abstract evaluates hourly, roughly 0.5 km radar-based QPE for ten extreme rain events in the Kansas City metropolitan area.
  • Two polarimetric algorithms based on specific attenuation and specific differential phase outperform the conventional reflectivity-only approach.
  • The paper also shows tradeoffs: KDP-based estimates reduce scatter but can underestimate high rain rates, while attenuation-based estimates are less biased but noisier.

Monthly Weather Review (AMS)

Ensemble Forecast Sensitivity to Observations (EFSO) Applied to All-sky Infrared Radiance Observations

Why it matters: It gives a same-week, abstract-supported view of how much geostationary all-sky IR observations help convective-scale forecasts.

  • The abstract applies EFSO to convective-scale data assimilation for a mesoscale convective system and a tropical cyclone.
  • In both cases, all-sky infrared radiances have more beneficial forecast impacts than the conventional observations examined in the study.
  • Cloudy-sky radiances are especially valuable, and the impacts depend on the thinning interval used for those satellite observations.

Atmosphere (MDPI)

Regional Evolution of the Meteosat Solar and Infrared Spectra (2005–2024) Linked to Cloud Cover and Ocean Surface

Why it matters: This is one of the material gains from the expanded source list: a newly captured MDPI Atmosphere paper built around long geostationary satellite records.

  • The abstract analyzes 2005–2024 Meteosat radiances and reports decreasing reflected solar radiation together with slightly increasing thermal infrared output.
  • The authors link the solar-domain decline mainly to reduced low-level cloud cover inside the Meteosat field of view, while also discussing CO2 and humidity effects.
  • Regional sector analysis connects cloud-cover change, top-of-atmosphere radiative imbalance, and Atlantic sea-surface-temperature variability.

Weather and Forecasting (AMS)

Examining the Impact of Different Uncrewed Aerial System (UAS) Sampling Strategies on Fog Forecasts in the Ohio Valley Using Observing System Simulation Experiments (OSSEs)

Why it matters: This adds a same-week Weather and Forecasting article that is genuinely forecast-oriented rather than a loose operations note.

  • The abstract uses observing system simulation experiments for three impactful Ohio Valley fog events to compare two UAS sampling strategies.
  • Both sampling strategies improve fog forecast skill after assimilation, showing that denser nocturnal boundary-layer observations can matter.
  • Profiling-style UAS networks improve temperature and dewpoint analyses aloft more strongly, which the authors tie to broader aviation-hazard forecasting value.

Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres (AGU)

Subseasonal Forecasting of Snow Cover and Cold Compound Extremes: Insights From MPAS-A Over Midlatitude East Asia

Why it matters: A strong NWP pick for the week because the abstract is explicit about lead-time skill, error sources, and model-physics sensitivity.

  • The abstract says MPAS-A can forecast rapid snow-cover expansion and temperature drops up to two pentads ahead, with detectable skill at three pentads.
  • Beyond four pentads the forecasts become unreliable, so the paper gives a clear upper bound on useful subseasonal skill in this setup.
  • Biases come mainly from underestimated snowfall, and the results are sensitive to the land-surface snow-cover-fraction scheme.

npj Climate and Atmospheric Science (Nature)

Global asymmetries in the moisture origins of atmospheric river precipitation

Why it matters: This is the other material gain from the expanded source list: a same-week npj paper that broadens the issue beyond the older AMS/AGU-only mix.

  • The abstract builds a 40-year global climatology by combining deep-learning atmospheric-river detection with Lagrangian moisture tracking.
  • It finds persistent west–east asymmetries: western continental boundaries rely more on extratropical moisture convergence, while eastern boundaries retain much larger tropical contributions.
  • The authors argue that direct tropical moisture delivery is not required for high-latitude atmospheric-river precipitation because moisture sources are progressively replaced along the poleward pathway.
