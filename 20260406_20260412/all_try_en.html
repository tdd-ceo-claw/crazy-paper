Monthly Weather Review (AMS)

Radar Data Assimilation with JEDI LETKF for Ensemble Forecasting of Hurricane Ida (2021) Using a HAFS-Like Configuration

Primary: RadarSecondary: Numerical Weather Prediction

Why it matters: A direct same-window example of radar observations materially improving hurricane ensemble prediction.

  • The abstract evaluates direct radar assimilation for Hurricane Ida inside the JEDI Local Ensemble Transform Kalman Filter framework.
  • Assimilating radial velocity improves the inner-core wind structure, reduces track spread during cycling, and improves landfall-location forecasts.
  • Reflectivity assimilation contributes more to rainfall skill, especially for extreme rainfall and outer-rainband precipitation before landfall.

Ensemble Forecast Sensitivity to Observations (EFSO) Applied to All-sky Infrared Radiance Observations

Primary: SatelliteSecondary: Numerical Weather Prediction

Why it matters: A clean abstract-supported view of how much geostationary all-sky IR observations help convective-scale forecasts.

  • The abstract applies EFSO to convective-scale data assimilation for a mesoscale convective system and a tropical cyclone.
  • In both cases, all-sky infrared radiances have more beneficial forecast impacts than the conventional observations examined in the study.
  • Cloudy-sky radiances are especially valuable, and the impacts depend on the thinning interval used for those satellite observations.

Journal of Hydrometeorology (AMS)

Evaluation of Radar-Derived Polarimetric Precipitation Estimates for Extreme Rain Events Using a Dense Network of Rain Gauges

Primary: Radar

Why it matters: A practical radar-QPE study with direct relevance to flood-oriented precipitation verification.

  • The abstract evaluates hourly radar-based QPE at roughly 0.5-km scale for ten extreme rain events in the Kansas City metropolitan area.
  • Two polarimetric algorithms based on specific attenuation and specific differential phase outperform the conventional reflectivity-only approach.
  • The paper also highlights tradeoffs: KDP-based estimates reduce scatter but can underestimate high rain rates, whereas attenuation-based estimates are less biased but noisier.

Atmosphere (MDPI)

Regional Evolution of the Meteosat Solar and Infrared Spectra (2005–2024) Linked to Cloud Cover and Ocean Surface

Primary: Satellite

Why it matters: One of the material gains from the expanded journal list: a long-record geostationary-satellite paper from MDPI Atmosphere.

  • The abstract analyzes 2005–2024 Meteosat radiances and reports decreasing reflected solar radiation together with slightly increasing thermal infrared output.
  • The authors connect the solar-domain decline mainly to reduced low-level cloud cover inside the Meteosat field of view, while also discussing CO2 and humidity effects.
  • Regional sector analysis links cloud-cover change, top-of-atmosphere radiative imbalance, and Atlantic sea-surface-temperature variability.

A Tropical Depression over the South China Sea in June 2025—Observational and Forecasting Aspects

Primary: Numerical Weather PredictionSecondary: AI

Why it matters: Useful because the abstract explicitly compares earlier AI alerts with conventional model value in local wind-impact forecasting.

  • The accessible abstract states that the paper documents upgrade decisions, center-location issues in a multiple-center situation, intensity forecasting, local wind impacts, and wind-structure analysis from dropsonde and wind-profiler data.
  • It says artificial-intelligence models provided earlier alerting of the tropical depression and its movement toward southern China.
  • The same abstract also says conventional physics-based models remained useful for forecasting the wind impacts in Hong Kong, so the paper fits best as a forecast-operations piece with an explicit AI angle.

Weather and Forecasting (AMS)

Examining the Impact of Different Uncrewed Aerial System (UAS) Sampling Strategies on Fog Forecasts in the Ohio Valley Using Observing System Simulation Experiments (OSSEs)

Primary: Numerical Weather Prediction

Why it matters: A genuinely forecast-oriented same-week Weather and Forecasting paper rather than a loose operations note.

  • The abstract uses OSSEs for three impactful Ohio Valley fog events to compare two UAS sampling strategies.
  • Both strategies improve fog forecast skill after assimilation, showing that denser nocturnal boundary-layer observations can matter.
  • A profiling-style UAS network improves temperature and dewpoint analyses aloft more strongly, which the authors tie to broader aviation-hazard forecasting value.

Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres (AGU)

Subseasonal Forecasting of Snow Cover and Cold Compound Extremes: Insights From MPAS-A Over Midlatitude East Asia

Primary: Numerical Weather Prediction

Why it matters: A strong NWP pick because the abstract is explicit about lead-time skill, error sources, and model-physics sensitivity.

  • The abstract says MPAS-A can forecast rapid snow-cover expansion and temperature drops up to two pentads ahead, with detectable skill at three pentads.
  • Beyond four pentads the forecasts become unreliable, giving a clear upper bound on useful subseasonal skill in this setup.
  • Biases come mainly from underestimated snowfall, and the results are sensitive to the land-surface snow-cover-fraction scheme.

npj Climate and Atmospheric Science (Nature)

Global asymmetries in the moisture origins of atmospheric river precipitation

Primary: All / UncategorizedSecondary: AI

Why it matters: A strong same-week cross-cutting paper that broadens the issue beyond the older AMS/AGU mix without pretending it is a classic forecast-model study.

  • The abstract builds a 40-year global climatology by combining deep-learning atmospheric-river detection with Lagrangian moisture tracking.
  • It finds persistent west–east asymmetries: western continental boundaries rely more on extratropical moisture convergence, while eastern boundaries retain much larger tropical contributions.
  • Because the AI component is a detection method rather than the main scientific question, this paper fits best in the all/unclassified bucket with a visible secondary AI label.
